Core Negotiation Concepts

These foundational concepts form the backbone of every successful negotiation strategy.

🎯

BATNA

Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement

Your BATNA is your power source in any negotiation. It represents what you will do if the current negotiation fails. The stronger your BATNA, the more leverage you have.

Example: Salary Negotiation If you have another job offer for $120K, that's your BATNA. You should never accept less than $120K from the current negotiation because you have a viable alternative.
  1. List all alternatives if this deal falls through
  2. Evaluate each alternative realistically
  3. Select the best alternative as your BATNA
  4. Improve your BATNA before negotiating
  5. Never reveal a weak BATNA
📊

ZOPA

Zone of Possible Agreement

The ZOPA is the range between each party's reservation point (walk-away price). If a ZOPA exists, a deal is possible. If it doesn't, no amount of negotiation will create agreement.

Max: $100K

Seller's Range
Wants: $110K
Min: $90K

ZOPA = $90K - $100K. Any deal in this range satisfies both parties' minimum requirements. Learn to identify ZOPAs in real-world scenarios.

⚓

Anchoring

First-Mover Advantage

The first number put on the table disproportionately influences the final outcome. Research consistently shows that the party who anchors first tends to achieve better results, provided the anchor is within a reasonable range.

When to Anchor First: When you have good information about the market value. When to let them anchor first: when you have very little information about what they might offer.
Anchoring in Practice Listing a house at $550K when you'd accept $500K anchors the buyer's thinking around $550K. Their counter-offer will likely be $510-530K rather than $450K. Apply decision principles from KeepRule.

Negotiation Frameworks

Harvard Method

Principled Negotiation

Developed by Roger Fisher and William Ury at Harvard, this framework separates people from problems and focuses on interests rather than positions.

The Four Principles:
  1. Separate people from the problem - Address relationship issues independently from substantive issues
  2. Focus on interests, not positions - "Why do you want that?" reveals underlying needs
  3. Generate options for mutual gain - Brainstorm before deciding
  4. Use objective criteria - Market data, precedent, expert opinion
Value Creation

Logrolling

Trading concessions on issues you value less for gains on issues you value more. This is the essence of creating value in negotiation - turning a fixed pie into a larger one.

Example: Employment Negotiation You care more about remote work flexibility (high value to you, low cost to employer). Employer cares more about start date (high value to them, low cost to you). Trade: accept their preferred start date in exchange for 3 days remote/week.

Defensive Tactics

Protect yourself from manipulative negotiation techniques:

  • Flinching: React visibly to their first offer to signal it's too extreme
  • Bracketing: If your target is $100K and they offer $80K, counter at $120K
  • Nibbling: Ask for small concessions after main terms are agreed
  • The Crunch: "You'll have to do better than that" without specifying how
  • Good Cop/Bad Cop: Recognize when teams play roles to pressure you

Collaborative Tactics

Build relationships and create joint value:

  • Active Listening: Paraphrase their points to show understanding
  • Labeling Emotions: "It seems like you're concerned about..." validates feelings
  • Calibrated Questions: "How am I supposed to do that?" shifts problem-solving to them
  • Strategic Empathy: Understand their perspective without agreeing
  • Mirroring: Repeat the last 1-3 words to encourage elaboration
🧠

Psychological Principles

Understand the psychology behind negotiation:

  • Reciprocity: Give something to get something in return
  • Loss Aversion: People fear losses 2x more than they value equivalent gains
  • Scarcity: Limited availability increases perceived value
  • Social Proof: "Others have agreed to similar terms..."
  • Commitment: Small yeses lead to bigger yeses

Negotiation Preparation Checklist

Before the Negotiation

  1. Research thoroughly - Know the market, their constraints, and alternatives
  2. Define your BATNA - What will you do if no agreement is reached?
  3. Set your reservation point - The worst deal you would accept
  4. Establish your target - The ideal outcome you're aiming for
  5. Prepare your opening - First offer that anchors the negotiation
  6. Identify their interests - Why they want what they want
  7. Plan your concessions - What you can give up and in what order
  8. Prepare for objections - Anticipate and rehearse responses

Negotiation Decision Framework

5 Questions Before Any Negotiation:
  1. What are my non-negotiable requirements?
  2. What am I willing to trade away?
  3. What does the other side truly need?
  4. Where can we create mutual value?
  5. What is my walk-away point?

Negotiation Styles Assessment

Understanding your natural negotiation style helps you adapt strategically to different situations.

🏆

Competitive Style

You prioritize your own outcomes. Effective when the relationship is less important than the result, but can damage long-term partnerships.

Best for: One-time transactions, clear win-lose scenarios, when you have strong leverage.

Risk: Burning bridges, missing creative solutions that benefit both parties.

🤝

Collaborative Style

You seek outcomes that satisfy both parties. Takes more time but creates lasting agreements and stronger relationships.

Best for: Ongoing relationships, complex multi-issue negotiations, when trust matters.

Risk: Takes longer, can be exploited by competitive negotiators if you are not careful.

⚖

Compromising Style

You split the difference. Quick and fair-feeling, but may leave value on the table that creative problem-solving could capture.

Best for: Time pressure, moderately important issues, when parties have equal power.

Risk: Settling for mediocre outcomes when better solutions exist. Use decision principles to evaluate.
